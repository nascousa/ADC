# Memo Humanize

Use this skill for class memos, security briefs, and report-style assignments that need a more natural voice without losing structure.

## Common Requests
- "Make this memo sound less stiff."
- "Keep the same structure, but rewrite the wording."
- "Remove the template language without changing the facts."
- "Make this read like a real class submission."

## Goal
- Keep the memo/report content accurate and complete.
- Make the wording sound like a thoughtful student, not a polished model output.
- Reduce over-structured phrasing, repetitive transitions, and obvious template language.

## Use When
- The assignment is formal but should still sound like your own writing.
- The draft has too many framework phrases or repeated lead-ins.
- The writing is technically correct but feels stiff.
- You want the result to stay readable in a PDF submission.

## What to Preserve
- Facts, sources, citations, links, and technical meaning.
- Headings, section order, rubric alignment, and memo/report structure.
- The document’s overall argument and recommendations.

## What to Change
- Replace phrases like "this memo revisits" or "the key result is" with plainer wording.
- Cut down repeated signposting such as "first," "second," "finally," when it sounds mechanical.
- Keep transitions simple and avoid over-explaining the structure of the document.
- Use direct language when a shorter sentence says the same thing.
- Keep the tone academic, but not stiff.

## Editing Procedure
1. Read the whole memo and find the parts that sound over-polished or too generic.
2. Keep the structure unless the structure itself is the problem.
3. Rewrite the most AI-like sentences first.
4. Make sure every claim still matches the source material.
5. Leave links and citations untouched unless the user asks for formatting changes.
6. Do a final tone pass to make it sound like a real class submission.

## Style Rules
- Use English only.
- Prefer concrete wording over promotional wording.
- Avoid em dashes when commas or periods work better.
- Do not add fake personality or exaggerated confidence.
- Do not invent facts, examples, or citations.
- Keep the writing natural, not chatty.

## Good Examples
- "This environment uses local endpoints and remote VM access."
- "The main risk is that a session can look legitimate even when it is not."
- "A gradual rollout makes more sense than a hard block on day one."

## Bad Examples
- "This memo serves as a testament to the transformative potential of..."
- "At its core, the value proposition is clear..."
- "This document revisits the same emerging environment..."

## Best Fit For This Workspace
- Assignment 1 and Assignment 2 memos.
- Report-style coursework that needs to sound less AI-generated.
- Any draft where structure is already good but the wording feels stiff.
