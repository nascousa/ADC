# Humanize Writing

Use this skill when a draft needs to sound less like a template and more like a real person wrote it.

## Common Requests
- "Make this sound less AI-generated."
- "Keep the content, but rewrite the wording."
- "Make this read more naturally for class."
- "Keep the structure, but make it sound human."

## Goal
- Keep the meaning, facts, and structure.
- Remove AI-sounding phrasing, repetitive transitions, and over-polished wording.
- Make the writing feel direct, natural, and believable for class submissions.

## Use When
- A memo, brief, or report feels too formal or generic.
- The text repeats the same sentence openings or transition words.
- The draft sounds like it was generated instead of written.
- You want the writing to stay academic but still sound human.

## What to Preserve
- Facts, names, dates, links, citations, and technical terms.
- Headings, list order, and document structure unless the user asks otherwise.
- The author’s intended meaning and level of certainty.

## What to Change
- Replace inflated phrases like "it is important to note" or "this memo revisits" with simpler language.
- Vary sentence length so the rhythm sounds natural.
- Use active voice when it is clearer.
- Avoid repeated patterns like "This aligns with...", "The key result is...", or "In order to...".
- Remove unnecessary filler, hedging, and chatbot-style signposting.
- Keep transitions simple and do not over-explain the writing process.

## Editing Procedure
1. Read the whole draft and identify the parts that sound too polished or repetitive.
2. Keep the structure unless the structure itself is the problem.
3. Rewrite only the sentences that sound artificial.
4. Use more direct wording and fewer stock phrases.
5. Check that URLs, citations, and technical details still match the source.
6. Do a final pass for tone: it should sound like a careful student, not a brochure.

## Style Rules
- Use English only.
- Prefer concrete wording over promotional wording.
- Keep the tone friendly and approachable, but still academic enough for class.
- Do not add fake personality, exaggeration, or dramatic language.
- Avoid em dashes when a comma or period works better.
- Do not invent facts or sources.

## Good Examples
- "This environment has three practical layers." 
- "The main risk is that digital input can look legitimate even when it is not." 
- "A staged rollout makes more sense than a hard lockout on day one."

## Bad Examples
- "This document serves as a testament to the transformative nature of..."
- "At its core, the value proposition is clear..."
- "This memo revisits the same emerging environment..."

## Best Fit For This Workspace
- Assignment writing for CSS539.
- Discussion and assignment responses for CSSSKL594.
- Drafts based on technical or project work that need to sound more human.
